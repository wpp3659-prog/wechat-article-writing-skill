# Article Frameworks

Use one primary framework. Blend lightly only when the topic truly needs it.

## Opinion

Use for abstract themes, value judgments, rebuttals, and "我认为..." topics.

Structure:

1. Opening: state a sharp or counterintuitive judgment.
2. Why this matters: describe the reader's familiar scene or misconception.
3. Main argument: give 2-3 reasons, each with examples or reasoning.
4. Counterargument: give the strongest opposing view, then explain the boundary.
5. Closing: leave a firm but human ending, then invite discussion.

Best for: hot takes, personal views, culture, workplace, education, AI, business trends.

## Practical Guide

Use for how-to, problem solving, methods, tools, and experience sharing.

Structure:

1. Opening: name the reader's problem and promise a useful takeaway.
2. Diagnosis: explain why common solutions fail.
3. Method: present 3-5 steps or principles.
4. Example: show the method in a concrete scenario.
5. Closing: summarize the core method in one sentence and prompt action.

Best for: tutorials, productivity, learning methods, business operations, personal growth.

## Story

Use for people, events, emotional topics, and lessons learned from a narrative.

Structure:

1. Opening: start from a scene, conflict, or surprising result.
2. Background: explain who, where, when, and what changed.
3. Turning point: spend the most detail here.
4. Insight: rise from story to meaning.
5. Closing: return to the opening image or unresolved question.

Best for: case studies, founder stories, failures, personal experiences, trend stories.

## Industry Analysis

Use for news, trends, technology, policy, product releases, or market shifts.

Structure:

1. Opening: explain what happened and why it matters.
2. Surface layer: what most people are saying.
3. Deeper layer: what is easy to miss.
4. Impact: short-term and long-term consequences.
5. Judgment: a clear prediction or decision frame.

Use web search for current facts. Do not invent statistics or market claims.

## Personal Reflection

Use when the topic is a personal observation, life lesson, emotion, or belief shift.

Structure:

1. Opening: a small honest scene or sentence.
2. Observation: what triggered the reflection.
3. Change: what the writer used to believe and what changed.
4. Meaning: what readers can borrow from this experience.
5. Closing: quiet, not over-polished.

Best for: self-growth, work-life, relationships, creator notes, year-end or milestone essays.

## Product Or Service Article

Use when the topic promotes, explains, or positions a product, service, course, tool, or company.

Structure:

1. Opening: start with the user's pain or use case, not the product.
2. Problem: make the cost of the old way concrete.
3. Solution: introduce the product as a response.
4. Proof: use real features, cases, screenshots, testimonials, or measurable outcomes if provided.
5. CTA: invite consultation, trial, comment, or next step.

Never invent proof. If proof is missing, write an editor note for the user to insert it.

## List Or Roundup

Use for recommendations, checklists, mistakes, tools, lessons, and "N 个..." topics.

Structure:

1. Opening: explain what readers will get.
2. List items: each item should have a name, explanation, scenario, and takeaway.
3. Contrast: compare which item fits which reader.
4. Closing: recommend where to start.

Keep item lengths varied to avoid a mechanical feeling.
